Compare the architectural tradeoffs between microservices and modular monoliths for a startup with 5 engineers.

A
Model A · claude-3.5-sonnet · 14:20:03

Great question. For a 5-person startup, the architecture decision isn't just technical — it's organizational. Let me break down the key tradeoffs:

Microservices offer independent deployability and technology diversity, but they impose a massive operational overhead that small teams struggle with. You're looking at:

  • Service mesh complexity — each service needs health checks, circuit breakers, and observability
  • Distributed debugging — tracing a request across 8 services when you have 5 engineers is painful
  • Deployment pipeline overhead — maintaining CI/CD for each service multiplies DevOps work

A modular monolith gives you clean separation via module boundaries and well-defined internal APIs, without the network overhead. You can always extract services later when your team — and your understanding of domain boundaries — matures.

My recommendation: start monolith, enforce module boundaries with ArchUnit or equivalent, and extract only when you hit genuine scaling bottlenecks.

Model B · gpt-4o · 14:20:05

I largely agree with Model A's framing but want to push back on one point: the "extract later" strategy sounds pragmatic but often fails in practice.

Here's why: once you build a monolith, even a "modular" one, teams develop hidden coupling through shared databases, in-process calls that bypass module APIs, and shared utility libraries. The cost of extraction grows exponentially with codebase age.

A more nuanced approach for a 5-person team:

  • Use a modular monolith as your deployment unit — single binary, single database
  • But design your inter-module communication as if it were async messaging from day one
  • Enforce that modules only communicate via events or well-typed command/query interfaces
  • When extraction time comes, you swap in-memory event bus for NATS or Kafka

This gives you the deployment simplicity of a monolith with the architectural readiness for future decomposition.
